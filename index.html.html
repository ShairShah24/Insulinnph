Insulin NPH (Neutral Protamine Hagedorn)

A comprehensive guide to pharmacology, dosing, mechanism, and clinical use

Generic Information

Generic Name

Insulin NPH (Neutral Protamine Hagedorn) or isophane insulin

Brand Names

Humulin N (Eli Lilly), Novolin N (Novo Nordisk), Insulatard (Sanofi), Gensulin N

Classification

Intermediate-acting insulin (duration: 14–24 hours)

Regulatory Status

WHO Essential Medicine, Prescription-only in the US/EU

Chemistry

Insulin NPH is a suspension of recombinant human insulin complexed with protamine and zinc at neutral pH (7.0–7.5).

Key Chemical Properties

  • Protamine source: Basic protein derived from salmon sperm
  • Appearance: Cloudy suspension due to crystalline microstructure
  • Formulation ratio: Protamine to insulin ratio of 1:5–1:10 to delay absorption
  • Preparation: Requires thorough resuspension before administration

Important Note

NPH insulin should never be administered intravenously due to the risk of severe adverse reactions from the protamine component.

Pharmacokinetics (LADME)

Parameter Value Influencing Factors
Liberation Slow dissolution of insulin-protamine-zinc crystals at subcutaneous site Injection technique, tissue characteristics
Absorption Onset: 1–3 hours; Peak: 4–8 hours; Duration: 14–24 hours Injection site, exercise, resuspension technique
Distribution Rapid extracellular distribution; does not cross placenta Body composition, circulatory status
Metabolism Primarily hepatic (60–80%), also renal and muscular Liver and kidney function
Excretion Renal (30–80% as metabolites) Renal function, glomerular filtration rate

Clinical Significance

Improper resuspension of NPH insulin before injection can increase pharmacokinetic variability by up to 23% (measured by AUC). This is why it's essential to roll the vial or pen between hands until the suspension appears uniformly cloudy.

Indications and Dosing

Approved Uses

  • Type 1 diabetes (as basal insulin component)
  • Type 2 diabetes (when oral hypoglycemic agents fail to achieve glycemic control)
  • Gestational diabetes mellitus (when insulin therapy is required)

Dosing Guidelines

Population Dosing Administration
Adults (Type 1) 0.4–1.0 units/kg/day, typically split twice daily Inject subcutaneously 15 mins pre-meal
Adults (Type 2) Start at 0.2 units/kg/day, titrate based on response Inject subcutaneously, usually with breakfast and dinner
Children (≥12 years) 0.5–1.0 units/kg/day Adjust based on growth, activity, and dietary patterns

Typical Dosing Schedule

For twice-daily administration:

  • Morning dose: 2/3 of total daily dose (with ratio of 1:2 rapid-acting insulin to NPH)
  • Evening dose: 1/3 of total daily dose (with ratio of 1:1 rapid-acting insulin to NPH)

Mechanism of Action

Glucose Regulation

Insulin NPH binds to insulin receptors on target cells, activating a signaling cascade that promotes GLUT4 translocation to the cell membrane, facilitating cellular glucose uptake.

Metabolic Effects

Liver

  • Inhibits gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis
  • Promotes glycogen synthesis

Muscle

  • Stimulates glycogen synthesis
  • Enhances protein synthesis
  • Facilitates amino acid uptake

Adipose Tissue

  • Enhances triglyceride storage
  • Suppresses lipolysis
  • Inhibits release of free fatty acids

Time-Action Profile

The intermediate action profile of NPH insulin makes it suitable as a basal insulin, providing background insulin coverage between meals and overnight. Its peak effect typically occurs between 4-8 hours after administration, which requires careful meal planning to prevent hypoglycemia.

Side Effects & Contraindications

Common Adverse Effects

  • Hypoglycemia (most frequent)
  • Weight gain
  • Injection site lipodystrophy or lipoatrophy

Rare / Serious Reactions

  • Systemic allergic reactions (urticaria, bronchospasm, hypotension)
  • Insulin edema syndrome
  • Antibody formation (can alter pharmacokinetics)

Contraindications

  • Known hypersensitivity to insulin NPH or protamine
  • Hypoglycemia (do not administer during episodes)

Clinical Tip

To minimize lipodystrophy, rotate injection sites regularly. For patients with recurrent nocturnal hypoglycemia, consider switching to insulin glargine or detemir.

Stability and Storage

Unopened Vials/Pens

  • Refrigerate at 2–8°C (36–46°F)
  • Keep in original packaging
  • Stable until expiration date
  • Avoid freezing

In-Use Vials/Pens

  • Stable at 15–30°C (59–86°F) for 28 days
  • Do not refrigerate after opening
  • Keep away from direct sunlight
  • Do not store with needle attached

Emergency Handling

If exposed to temperatures >86°F (30°C), the insulin may be used temporarily but blood glucose should be monitored closely and the insulin replaced as soon as possible. Do not use if frozen, discolored, or containing particles.

Market Challenges in Pakistan

  • High cost: Insulin analogs cost 3-4 times more than human insulin
  • Supply chain gaps: Particularly in rural areas and public sector facilities
  • Import dependence: 87.5% of market dominated by multinational brands
  • Public sector availability: Less than 50% compared to WHO target of 80%

Regulatory Developments

The Drug Regulatory Authority of Pakistan (DRAP) is promoting biosimilars and local manufacturing to improve accessibility. The 2022 approval of Gan & Lee's insulin glargine represents a step toward more affordable alternatives.
